1.1 WHAT IS INVESTLAB?
──────────────────────

  InvestLab is an investment-learning module embedded inside BudgetBuddy, a
  MERN-stack student finance platform. It provides students with a simulated
  paper trading environment where they can learn how markets work without
  risking real money.

  Key Characteristics:
  - Paper trading with $10,000 simulated cash
  - Real-time market data from Finnhub (actual stock prices)
  - Investment readiness scoring based on the student's existing financial data
  - Trade tracking and portfolio valuation
  - NOT a stock trading platform — no real money, no brokerage integration

1.2 WHY DOES INVESTLAB EXIST INSIDE BUDGETBUDDY?
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  BudgetBuddy already tracks a student's:
  - Income and expenses (Transaction model)
  - Budget limits per category (Budget model)
  - Recurring subscriptions (subscriptionService)
  - Spending patterns over time

  InvestLab leverages ALL of this existing data to determine if a student
  is financially "ready" to start investing. This creates a natural progression:

    Step 1: Track your expenses          (existing)
    Step 2: Set and follow budgets       (existing)
    Step 3: Understand your readiness    (InvestLab - readiness score)
    Step 4: Practice with paper trading  (InvestLab - portfolio/trades)
    Step 5: Learn from real market data  (InvestLab - Finnhub WebSocket)

  The readiness score bridges the gap between "tracking money" and
  "growing money" — which is the natural next step for any student.

1.3 WHY IS IT AN INVESTMENT-LEARNING MODULE AND NOT A STOCK TRADING PLATFORM?
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Reasons:
  1. Legal: Real stock trading requires brokerage licenses, SEC compliance,
     KYC verification, and regulatory approvals. This is a student project.

  2. Target Audience: Students typically don't have investable capital. They
     need to LEARN how markets work before committing real money.

  3. Safety: Paper trading eliminates financial risk while preserving the
     learning experience. A student who loses $5,000 in paper money learns
     the same lesson as losing real money — without the consequences.

  4. Integration: By embedding it inside BudgetBuddy, we can use real
     financial data (expenses, budgets, subscriptions) to teach students
     WHEN they're ready to invest — not just HOW.

  5. Scope: A real trading platform would require order books, market
     makers, settlement systems, and compliance infrastructure. InvestLab
     focuses purely on education.

1.4 PROBLEMS SOLVED
────────────────────

  Problem 1: Students don't know WHEN they're ready to invest
  Solution:  Readiness scoring engine that analyzes their actual finances

  Problem 2: Students are afraid to try investing
  Solution:  Paper trading with $10,000 simulated cash — zero risk

  Problem 3: Students don't understand real-time markets
  Solution:  Live market data via Finnhub WebSocket — real prices, real time

  Problem 4: Students can't track their investment learning progress
  Solution:  Trade history, portfolio valuation, P&L tracking

  Problem 5: Financial education apps feel disconnected from personal finance
  Solution:  InvestLab uses the student's OWN expense/budget data for readiness

1.5 STUDENT USE CASES
─────────────────────

  Use Case 1: "Am I Ready?"
    A student checks their readiness score. They have a savings rate of 30%,
    all budgets within limits, and low subscription burden. Score: 72 ("Ready").
    They now feel confident to explore paper trading.

  Use Case 2: "Practice Trading"
    A student buys 5 shares of AAPL at $300. The next day, AAPL goes to $305.
    Their portfolio shows a $25 unrealized gain. They learn about P&L without
    losing real money.

  Use Case 3: "Learn from Mistakes"
    A student buys TSLA at $400. It drops to $380. They sell in panic at a loss.
    Their trade history shows the mistake. They learn about holding vs. selling.

  Use Case 4: "Market Awareness"
    Through real-time price updates, students learn that markets move constantly.
    They see NVDA spike 2% in a day and understand volatility.

1.6 TECHNICAL GOALS (DAY 1)
────────────────────────────

  1. Build real-time market data infrastructure (Finnhub WebSocket)
  2. Design and create MongoDB models (Portfolio, Trade, PortfolioSnapshot)
  3. Implement Redis Pub/Sub architecture for scalable price distribution
  4. Integrate Socket.io for real-time client communication
  5. Build REST APIs for readiness, portfolio, buy, sell, trades
  6. Create a reusable readiness scoring engine
  7. Create a reusable portfolio valuation service
  8. Auto-provision portfolios on user signup

2.2 LAYER-BY-LAYER EXPLANATION
──────────────────────────────

  LAYER 1: Finnhub WebSocket
  ──────────────────────────
  What:    External WebSocket API providing real-time trade data
  Why:     We need actual market prices for paper trading to feel real
  Data:    Sends trade events with symbol, price, volume, timestamp
  Note:    Free tier supports up to 50 symbols (we use 6)

  LAYER 2: finnhubService
  ───────────────────────
  What:    Our WebSocket client that connects to Finnhub
  Why:     Centralizes connection management, caching, and reconnection
  Key:     Maintains a latestPrices Map (in-memory cache)
  Output:  Emits 'priceUpdate' events via a shared EventEmitter

  LAYER 3: EventEmitter
  ─────────────────────
  What:    Node.js built-in event system
  Why:     Decouples the WebSocket client from downstream consumers
  Benefit: finnhubService doesn't need to know WHO listens to its events.
           Today it's marketDataGateway. Tomorrow it could be an analytics
           pipeline, a logging system, or a BullMQ job producer.

  LAYER 4: marketDataGateway
  ──────────────────────────
  What:    Bridges EventEmitter events to Redis Pub/Sub
  Why:     Decouples market data ingestion from Socket.io delivery
  Key:     Uses a dedicated Redis publisher connection

  LAYER 5: Redis Pub/Sub
  ──────────────────────
  What:    Redis publish/subscribe messaging
  Why:     Enables horizontal scaling (multiple server instances)
  Channel: 'market:prices'
  Key:     Separate connections for publish and subscribe (Redis requirement)

  LAYER 6: Socket.io
  ──────────────────
  What:    Real-time bidirectional communication with clients
  Why:     Clients need live price updates without polling
  Events:  'priceSnapshot' (on connect), 'priceUpdate' (on each price change)

2.3 WHY THIS ARCHITECTURE? (ALTERNATIVES CONSIDERED)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  REJECTED: Finnhub → Direct Socket.io
  ─────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: Tightly couples data source to delivery mechanism
  Problem: Cannot scale to multiple server instances
  Problem: Cannot add other consumers (analytics, logging) without code changes
  Problem: If Socket.io goes down, we lose data ingestion too

  REJECTED: Polling REST API for prices
  ──────────────────────────────────────
  Problem: High latency (seconds or minutes between updates)
  Problem: Wasted HTTP requests when prices haven't changed
  Problem: Higher server load from repeated connections

  REJECTED: Server-Sent Events (SSE)
  ───────────────────────────────────
  Problem: Unidirectional only (server → client)
  Problem: No built-in reconnection handling
  Problem: Less ecosystem support than Socket.io

  CHOSEN: Finnhub WS → EventEmitter → Redis Pub/Sub → Socket.io
  ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  Benefit: Each layer is independently scalable
  Benefit: Redis enables multi-server architectures
  Benefit: EventEmitter allows multiple subscribers
  Benefit: Socket.io handles reconnection, rooms, namespaces
  Benefit: Clean separation of concerns

4.2 PORTFOLIO SCHEMA
────────────────────

  Collection: portfolios

  Fields:
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬────────────────────────┐
  │ Field             │ Type                 │ Constraints            │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
  │ _id               │ ObjectId (auto)      │ Primary key            │
  │ userId            │ ObjectId (ref: User) │ Required, Unique       │
  │ cashBalance       │ Number               │ Default: 10000         │
  │ holdings          │ Array of subdocs     │ Each: {symbol, qty,    │
  │                   │                      │  avgBuyPrice}          │
  │ createdAt         │ Date (auto)          │ timestamps: true       │
  │ updatedAt         │ Date (auto)          │ timestamps: true       │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────┴────────────────────────┘

  Holdings Subdocument:
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬────────────────────────┐
  │ Field             │ Type                 │ Constraints            │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
  │ symbol            │ String               │ Required               │
  │ quantity          │ Number               │ Required, min: 0       │
  │ avgBuyPrice       │ Number               │ Required, min: 0       │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────┴────────────────────────┘

  Note: Holdings subdocument has { _id: false } to avoid generating
  unnecessary ObjectIds for each holding entry.

  Purpose:
  - Tracks a user's paper trading account
  - One portfolio per user (unique constraint on userId)
  - cashBalance starts at $10,000 (simulated money)
  - holdings array stores current stock positions with weighted average cost

  Relationships:
  - One-to-One with User (each user has exactly one portfolio)
  - userId references the User collection

  Indexes:
  - Unique index on userId (enforced by unique: true in schema)
  - This prevents duplicate portfolios and enables O(1) lookups by user

  Sample Document:
  {
    "_id": ObjectId("6a318866604474ecdd409bba"),
    "userId": ObjectId("6a318866604474ecdd409bb9"),
    "cashBalance": 8498.70,
    "holdings": [

        "symbol": "AAPL",

  Collection: trades

  Fields:
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬────────────────────────┐
  │ Field             │ Type                 │ Constraints            │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼────────────────────────┤
  │ _id               │ ObjectId (auto)      │ Primary key            │
  │ userId            │ ObjectId (ref: User) │ Required               │
  │ symbol            │ String               │ Required               │
  │ type              │ String               │ Required, enum:        │
  │                   │                      │ ['buy', 'sell']        │
  │ quantity          │ Number               │ Required, min: 1       │
  │ price             │ Number               │ Required, min: 0       │
  │ total             │ Number               │ Required, min: 0       │
  │ timestamp         │ Date                 │ Default: Date.now      │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────┴────────────────────────┘

  Purpose:
  - Immutable audit trail of every buy/sell action
  - Never modified or deleted — append-only
  - Separate from Portfolio for clean separation of concerns
  - Enables trade history display, analytics, and learning

  Relationships:
  - Many-to-One with User (each user has many trades)
  - userId references the User collection

  Indexes:
  - Compound index: { userId: 1, timestamp: -1 }
  - This enables efficient per-user trade history sorted by most recent

  Sample Document:
  {
    "_id": ObjectId("6a31888e604474ecdd409bbb"),
    "userId": ObjectId("6a318866604474ecdd409bb9"),
    "symbol": "AAPL",
    "type": "buy",
    "quantity": 5,

    "total": 1501.30,

================================================================================
                     SECTION 5: DBMS CONCEPTS USED
================================================================================

5.1 COLLECTIONS
───────────────

  Definition:
    In MongoDB, a collection is the equivalent of a table in relational
    databases. It stores a group of related documents. Collections do not
    enforce a fixed schema — each document can have different fields.

  Where Used:
    - portfolios: Stores user paper trading portfolios
    - trades: Stores buy/sell trade records
    - portfoliosnapshots: Stores point-in-time portfolio values

  Why Used:
    MongoDB collections provide flexible schema design, which is ideal
    for a rapidly evolving feature like InvestLab where fields may change.

  Interview Answer:
    "I used MongoDB collections to store portfolio, trade, and snapshot
    data. Collections in MongoDB are schema-flexible, so I could iterate
    on the data model quickly during development. I still enforced
    structure using Mongoose schemas for type safety and validation."

5.2 DOCUMENTS
─────────────

  Definition:
    A document is a single record in a MongoDB collection, stored as
    BSON (Binary JSON). Each document has a unique _id field.

  Where Used:
    - Each Portfolio document represents one user's paper trading account
    - Each Trade document represents one buy/sell action
    - Each PortfolioSnapshot represents one point-in-time value capture

  Why Used:
    Documents map naturally to JavaScript objects, making them ideal
    for a Node.js/Express backend. No ORM translation layer needed.

5.3 OBJECTID
─────────────

  Definition:
    ObjectId is MongoDB's default 12-byte unique identifier for documents.
    It contains a timestamp, machine identifier, process ID, and counter.

  Where Used:
    - _id field on every document (auto-generated)
    - userId field on Portfolio, Trade, PortfolioSnapshot (references)

  Why Used:
    - Globally unique without coordination (no auto-increment locks)
    - Timestamp-embedded (can extract creation time from _id)
    - Efficient for indexing

  Interview Answer:
    "ObjectIds are globally unique identifiers that MongoDB generates
    automatically. They're 12 bytes and contain a timestamp component,
    which means I can sort by _id to get chronological order without
    a separate timestamp field. I used ObjectId references for userId
    to create relationships between collections."

5.4 REFERENCES (ref)
────────────────────

  Definition:
    In Mongoose, the 'ref' property on an ObjectId field indicates which
    collection the ID references. This enables population (joins).

  Where Used:
    - Portfolio.userId → ref: 'User'
    - Trade.userId → ref: 'User'
    - PortfolioSnapshot.userId → ref: 'User'

  Why Used:
    - Establishes logical relationships between collections
    - Enables Mongoose .populate() for joins when needed
    - Documents which collection owns the referenced document

  Interview Answer:
    "I used Mongoose ref to create relationships between InvestLab
    collections and the User collection. For example, Portfolio.userId
    references the User model. This lets me use .populate('userId')
    to join user data when needed, similar to a foreign key in SQL."

5.5 RELATIONSHIPS
─────────────────

  ONE-TO-ONE: User ↔ Portfolio
  ────────────────────────────
  Definition: Each user has exactly one portfolio.
  Implementation: unique: true constraint on Portfolio.userId
  Why: A paper trading account is per-user. Multiple portfolios
       would complicate the UX and business logic.

  ONE-TO-MANY: User → Trades
  ──────────────────────────
  Definition: Each user has many trades, but each trade belongs to one user.
  Implementation: Trade.userId references User._id (no unique constraint)
  Why: A user makes many buy/sell actions over time. Each trade is
       an immutable record — it's never updated or deleted.

  ONE-TO-MANY: User → PortfolioSnapshots
  ──────────────────────────────────────
  Definition: Each user has many snapshots over time.
  Implementation: PortfolioSnapshot.userId references User._id
  Why: Snapshots are time-series data — many data points per user.

  Interview Answer:
    "InvestLab uses both one-to-one and one-to-many relationships.
    User-to-Portfolio is one-to-one, enforced by a unique index on userId.
    User-to-Trade is one-to-many because each user can have hundreds of
    trades over time. I chose not to embed trades inside the Portfolio
    document because the trades array could grow unboundedly, which would
    hit MongoDB's 16MB document size limit."

5.6 INDEXING
────────────

  Definition:
    An index is a data structure that improves query performance by
    allowing MongoDB to find documents without scanning every document
    in a collection (similar to a book's index).

  Where Used:
    - Portfolio: unique index on userId (enforced by unique: true)
    - Trade: compound index { userId: 1, timestamp: -1 }
    - PortfolioSnapshot: compound index { userId: 1, timestamp: -1 }

  Why Used:
    - Portfolio unique index: O(1) lookup by user, prevents duplicates
    - Trade compound index: Efficient for "get trades for user X,
      sorted by newest first" — the most common query pattern
    - Snapshot compound index: Same pattern — time-series per user

  Interview Answer:
    "I created compound indexes on { userId: 1, timestamp: -1 } for
    trades and snapshots. This means MongoDB can satisfy the query
    'find all trades for user X, sorted by timestamp descending'
    entirely from the index without scanning the full collection.
    The '1' means ascending order on userId (for equality matching)
    and '-1' means descending order on timestamp (for sort order).
    This is called an ESR pattern — Equality, Sort, Range."

5.7 UNIQUE CONSTRAINTS
──────────────────────

  Definition:
    A unique constraint ensures no two documents in a collection can
    have the same value for a specific field or combination of fields.

  Where Used:
    - Portfolio.userId: unique: true
    - This ensures each user has AT MOST one portfolio

  Why Used:
    - Business rule: One paper trading account per user
    - Prevents bugs where multiple portfolios could be accidentally created
    - Acts as a database-level safety net

5.8 SCHEMA DESIGN: EMBEDDING VS REFERENCING
────────────────────────────────────────────

  DECISION: Holdings are EMBEDDED inside Portfolio (subdocument array)

  Why Embedded:
  - Holdings are always accessed together with the portfolio
  - A user has a small, bounded number of holdings (max 6 symbols)
  - No need to query holdings independently
  - Atomic updates (buy/sell modifies portfolio + holdings in one write)

  DECISION: Trades are in a SEPARATE COLLECTION (referenced)

  Why Referenced (not embedded):
  - Trade history grows unboundedly over time
  - MongoDB documents have a 16MB size limit
  - Trades need independent pagination and filtering
  - Trades are append-only (never modified)
  - Clean separation: Portfolio = current state, Trade = history

  Interview Answer:
    "I used embedding for holdings because they're always accessed with
    the portfolio, there's a small bounded number of them (max 6 stocks),
    and they need atomic updates with cash balance. I used a separate
    collection for trades because trade history grows unboundedly — a
    user could have thousands of trades. If I embedded trades inside
    Portfolio, the document would eventually hit MongoDB's 16MB limit.
    Additionally, trades need independent pagination which is hard with
    embedded arrays."

5.9 DENORMALIZATION VS NORMALIZATION
────────────────────────────────────

  Definition:
    - Normalization: Split data across tables/collections to avoid
      duplication (SQL-style). Requires joins for queries.
    - Denormalization: Duplicate some data to avoid joins and improve
      read performance.

  Where Used:
    - Trade.total is denormalized — it's price * quantity, which could
      be computed. But storing it avoids recalculation on every read.
    - Portfolio.holdings[].avgBuyPrice is maintained as a running weighted
      average — denormalized for instant access.

  Why:
    - In MongoDB, denormalization is common because joins ($lookup) are
      expensive compared to relational databases
    - Trade.total saves computation on trade history queries
    - avgBuyPrice saves recomputing from entire trade history

5.10 AGGREGATION OPPORTUNITIES
──────────────────────────────

  Definition:
    MongoDB's aggregation pipeline processes documents through stages
    like $match, $group, $sort, $project to compute derived values.

  Where Used in InvestLab:
    - getReadiness controller uses aggregation pipelines to compute:
      * Total monthly income: $match + $group + $sum
      * Total monthly expenses: $match + $group + $sum
      * Per-category expenses: $match + $group + $sum
      * Last month's expenses: $match + $group + $sum

  Why:
    - Aggregation runs on the database server, not in Node.js
    - More efficient than fetching all documents and computing in JS
    - Reduces network transfer (only aggregated results are returned)

  Interview Answer:
    "I used MongoDB aggregation pipelines in the readiness endpoint to
    compute monthly totals, category breakdowns, and month-over-month
    comparisons. The advantage is that aggregation runs on the MongoDB
    server — if I have 10,000 transactions, I don't transfer all of
    them to Node.js. I only get back the computed totals. This is
    O(n) on the server but O(1) for network transfer."

6.3 POST /api/investlab/buy
───────────────────────────

  Purpose: Execute a paper trade buy order.

  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Client → POST /api/investlab/buy                               │
  │  Body: { "symbol": "AAPL", "quantity": 5 }                     │
  └──────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  protect() middleware → verify JWT → attach req.user             │
  └──────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  CONTROLLER: buyStock()                                          │
  │                                                                  │
  │  VALIDATION:                                                     │
  │  1. Check symbol and quantity are present                        │
  │  2. Uppercase symbol, verify it's in SYMBOLS array               │
  │  3. Parse quantity as integer, check >= 1                        │
  │                                                                  │
  │  PRICE CHECK:                                                    │
  │  4. getLatestPrices() → check symbol has price data              │
  │  5. If no price → 503 "Market data may still be loading"        │
  │                                                                  │
  │  BALANCE CHECK:                                                  │
  │  6. Get or create portfolio                                      │
  │  7. total = price * quantity                                     │
  │  8. If cashBalance < total → 400 "Insufficient balance"         │
  │                                                                  │
  │  UPDATE HOLDINGS:                                                │
  │  9a. If existing holding for this symbol:                        │
  │      Weighted avg = (oldQty*oldAvg + newQty*newPrice) / totalQty │
  │  9b. If new symbol:                                              │
  │      Push new holding { symbol, quantity, avgBuyPrice: price }   │
  │                                                                  │
  │  10. Deduct total from cashBalance                               │
  │  11. portfolio.save()                                            │
  │                                                                  │
  │  RECORD TRADE:                                                   │
  │  12. Trade.create({ userId, symbol, type:'buy', qty, price })    │
  └──────┬───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
         ▼
  ┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  RESPONSE: 201 Created                                          │
  │  {                                                              │
  │    "message": "Bought 5 shares of AAPL at $300.26",             │
  │    "trade": { ... },                                            │
  │    "cashBalance": 8498.70                                       │
  │  }                                                              │
  └──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  Important: Weighted Average Buy Price
  ──────────────────────────────────────
  When a user buys the same stock multiple times at different prices,
  we calculate a weighted average:

  Example:
    Buy 5 AAPL at $300 → avgBuyPrice = $300
    Buy 3 AAPL at $310 → avgBuyPrice = (5*300 + 3*310) / 8 = $303.75

  This is standard practice in portfolio tracking (used by brokerages
  like Robinhood, Zerodha, etc.).

6.4 POST /api/investlab/sell
────────────────────────────

  Purpose: Execute a paper trade sell order.

  Flow is similar to buy, with these differences:
  1. Check user HOLDS the symbol (holdingIndex !== -1)
  2. Check user has ENOUGH shares (holding.quantity >= qty)
  3. SUBTRACT quantity from holding
  4. If quantity reaches 0, REMOVE the holding from array
  5. ADD total to cashBalance (instead of subtracting)
  6. Create Trade with type: 'sell'

  Error Cases:
  - 400 "Portfolio not found. Buy stocks first." (no portfolio)
  - 400 "You do not hold any AAPL shares" (symbol not in holdings)
  - 400 "Insufficient shares" (trying to sell more than owned)

================================================================================
                    SECTION 7: ROUTE LAYER DEEP DIVE
================================================================================

7.1 WHAT ARE ROUTES?
────────────────────

  Routes are URL-to-handler mappings. They define WHAT HTTP method and
  WHAT URL pattern triggers WHICH controller function. Routes are the
  "address book" of the API.

  In Express, routes are defined on a Router object:
    router.get('/readiness', getReadiness);
    router.post('/buy', buyStock);

  This means:
    GET http://localhost:5000/api/investlab/readiness → getReadiness()
    POST http://localhost:5000/api/investlab/buy → buyStock()

7.2 WHY DO ROUTES EXIST?
─────────────────────────

  Routes provide:
  1. URL Organization: Group related endpoints under a prefix
  2. Middleware Scoping: Apply middleware to a group of routes
  3. Separation of Concerns: URL mapping is separate from business logic
  4. Modularity: Each feature gets its own route file

7.3 HOW ROUTES CONNECT TO CONTROLLERS
──────────────────────────────────────

  The route file imports controller functions and maps them:

  File: routes/investlabRoutes.js

    const { protect } = require('../middleware/authMiddleware');
    const { getReadiness, getPortfolio, buyStock, sellStock, getTrades }
      = require('../controllers/investlabController');

    router.use(protect);    // Apply JWT auth to ALL routes below

    router.get('/readiness', getReadiness);
    router.get('/portfolio', getPortfolio);
    router.post('/buy', buyStock);
    router.post('/sell', sellStock);
    router.get('/trades', getTrades);

  Then server.js registers the router:

    app.use('/api/investlab', investlabRoutes);

  This creates the full URL path: /api/investlab/readiness, etc.

7.4 MIDDLEWARE EXECUTION ORDER
──────────────────────────────

  When a request hits GET /api/investlab/readiness:

  1. Express global middleware (runs for ALL routes):
     → cors()          : Check CORS headers
     → express.json()  : Parse JSON body
     → cookieParser()  : Parse cookies

  2. Router-level middleware (runs for /api/investlab/* only):
     → protect()       : Verify JWT, attach req.user

  3. Route handler:
     → getReadiness()  : Business logic

  Execution chain: cors → json → cookie → protect → getReadiness

7.5 JWT PROTECTION FLOW
────────────────────────

  The protect middleware (authMiddleware.js):

  1. Check if Authorization header exists and starts with 'Bearer'
  2. Extract the token: header.split(' ')[1]
  3. Verify token: jwt.verify(token, process.env.JWT_SECRET)
  4. Find user: User.findById(decoded.id).select('-password')
  5. Attach user to request: req.user = user
  6. Call next() to proceed to the controller

  If token is missing → 401 "No token"
  If token is invalid/expired → 401 "Not authorized"

  The controller then accesses req.user._id to scope all queries
  to the authenticated user.


================================================================================
                   SECTION 8: CONTROLLER LAYER DEEP DIVE
================================================================================

8.1 WHAT ARE CONTROLLERS?
─────────────────────────

  Controllers are functions that handle HTTP requests. They sit between
  routes (which define URLs) and services (which contain business logic).

  Controller responsibilities:
  1. Extract data from the request (req.body, req.query, req.user)
  2. Validate input data
  3. Call appropriate services
  4. Format and send the response

8.2 WHY BUSINESS LOGIC SHOULD NOT LIVE IN CONTROLLERS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Bad Pattern (fat controller):
    const buyStock = async (req, res) => {
      // Validation, price lookup, balance check, weighted average
      // calculation, portfolio update, trade creation — ALL HERE
    };

  Good Pattern (thin controller):
    const buyStock = async (req, res) => {
      // 1. Validate
      // 2. Call tradeService.executeBuy()  ← business logic
      // 3. Return response
    };

  Why thin controllers?
  1. Reusability: Services can be called from controllers, jobs, or tests
  2. Testability: Pure service functions are easy to unit test
  3. Maintainability: Changing business logic doesn't touch HTTP layer
  4. Readability: Each file has a single clear responsibility

  In our implementation, readinessEngine and portfolioValuationService are
  pure service functions. The buy/sell logic is still in the controller
  because it's tightly coupled to the HTTP request/response cycle
  (validation errors return specific HTTP status codes). This will be
  refactored into a tradeService in future iterations.

8.3 CONTROLLER: getReadiness
────────────────────────────

  Input:  req.user._id (from JWT middleware)
  Output: { score, status, breakdown, inputs }

  Validation: None (no user input needed)

  Service Calls:
  1. Transaction.aggregate() x3 (income, expenses, category expenses)
  2. Budget.find() (fetch user's budget limits)
  3. detectSubscriptions() (reuses existing subscription service)
  4. Transaction.aggregate() x1 (last month's expenses)
  5. calculateReadiness() (readiness engine service)

  Error Handling:
  - try/catch wraps entire function
  - 500 "Server error" for any unhandled exception

8.4 CONTROLLER: getPortfolio
────────────────────────────

  Input:  req.user._id
  Output: Portfolio with live valuation

  Validation: None

  Service Calls:
  1. Portfolio.findOne() (database lookup)
  2. Portfolio.create() (lazy init if not found)

                    SECTION 9: SERVICE LAYER DEEP DIVE
================================================================================

9.1 WHAT IS SERVICE-ORIENTED ARCHITECTURE?
──────────────────────────────────────────

  Service-oriented architecture (SOA) organizes code into services —
  modules that encapsulate specific business capabilities. Each service:

  1. Has a single responsibility
  2. Exposes a clean API (exported functions)
  3. Hides its internal implementation
  4. Can be tested independently
  5. Can be reused by multiple consumers

  In our codebase, services are separate .js files in the services/ folder.

9.2 WHY SERVICES WERE USED
───────────────────────────

  1. Reusability: portfolioValuationService is used by the controller
     today and will be used by BullMQ snapshot jobs tomorrow.

  2. Testability: readinessEngine is a pure function — you can test
     it with mock inputs without a database or HTTP server.

  3. Separation of Concerns: finnhubService handles WebSocket
     connections. marketDataGateway handles Redis publishing.
     Neither knows about the other's implementation.

  4. Modularity: Adding new features means adding new services,
     not modifying existing controller code.

9.3 SERVICE: finnhubService
───────────────────────────

  Purpose:
    Connect to Finnhub's real-time WebSocket API and maintain a
    cache of latest stock prices for 6 symbols.

  Data Flow:
    Finnhub sends: { type: 'trade', data: [{ s, p, v, t, c }, ...] }
                        ↓
    Parse and extract: symbol (s), price (p), volume (v), timestamp (t)
                        ↓
    Update latestPrices Map: { "AAPL": { price: 300.26, volume: 40, ... } }
                        ↓
    Emit 'priceUpdate' event via priceEmitter with changed prices

  Connection Lifecycle:
    1. startFinnhub() is called when server starts (server.js line 78)
    2. WebSocket connects to wss://ws.finnhub.io?token=<API_KEY>
    3. On 'open': Subscribe to 6 symbols via JSON messages
    4. On 'message': Parse trade data, update cache, emit events
    5. On 'close': Auto-reconnect with exponential backoff
    6. On 'error': Log error (close event will trigger reconnect)

  Reconnection Strategy:
    Exponential backoff: 1s → 2s → 4s → 8s → 16s → 30s (capped)

    Attempt 0 → wait 1 second
    Attempt 1 → wait 2 seconds
    Attempt 2 → wait 4 seconds
    Attempt 3 → wait 8 seconds
    Attempt 4 → wait 16 seconds
    Attempt 5+ → wait 30 seconds (MAX_RECONNECT_DELAY)

    Formula: delay = min(1000 * 2^attempt, 30000)

    On successful connect: reconnectAttempt resets to 0

  Why Exponential Backoff?
    - Prevents hammering the server if it's down
    - Gradually increases wait time to be respectful
    - Caps at 30 seconds to avoid waiting too long
    - Standard industry pattern (used by AWS, Google, etc.)

  Price Cache (latestPrices):
    - JavaScript Map object
    - Key: symbol string (e.g., "AAPL")
    - Value: { price, volume, timestamp }
    - getLatestPrices() returns Object.fromEntries(latestPrices)
    - Used by controllers for buy/sell price lookups
    - Used by Socket.io for initial priceSnapshot on connect

  EventEmitter Usage:
    - priceEmitter is a Node.js EventEmitter instance
    - Emits 'priceUpdate' event with changed price data
    - marketDataGateway listens to this event
    - Decouples finnhubService from its consumers
    - Future: analytics pipeline, alerting system could also listen

9.4 SERVICE: marketDataGateway
──────────────────────────────

  Purpose:
    Bridge EventEmitter events from finnhubService to Redis Pub/Sub.
    This is the decoupling layer between data ingestion and delivery.

  Redis Pub/Sub Flow:
    priceEmitter.on('priceUpdate', (updates) => {
      publisher.publish('market:prices', JSON.stringify({
        type: 'priceUpdate',
        data: updates,
        timestamp: Date.now()
      }));
    });

  Decoupling Benefits:
    Without gateway:  finnhubService → Socket.io (direct)
    With gateway:     finnhubService → EventEmitter → Redis → Socket.io

  Why the extra layer?

  1. Multiple Consumers: Redis Pub/Sub allows ANY subscriber to receive
     price updates — not just Socket.io. Future consumers include:
     - BullMQ workers for snapshots
     - Analytics pipelines
     - External microservices

  2. Horizontal Scaling: With Redis as the intermediary, you can run
     multiple Express servers. All of them subscribe to the same Redis
     channel and broadcast to their connected clients.

  3. Process Isolation: If Socket.io crashes, data ingestion continues.
     If Finnhub disconnects, Redis Pub/Sub still works (just no new data).

  4. Debugging: You can subscribe to the Redis channel with redis-cli
     to see raw market data without touching the application code:
       docker-compose exec redis redis-cli SUBSCRIBE market:prices

  Scalability Benefits:
    Single server:   finnhubService → eventEmitter → gateway → Redis → io
    Multiple servers: Same Redis → each server's io broadcasts to its clients

    This is the standard architecture used by production systems like
    trading platforms, chat applications, and real-time dashboards.

9.5 SERVICE: readinessEngine
────────────────────────────

  Scoring Formula:

  ┌──────────────────────┬────────┬──────────────────────────────┐
  │ Factor               │ Weight │ Calculation                  │
  ├──────────────────────┼────────┼──────────────────────────────┤
  │ Savings Rate          │ 40%    │ (savings / income) * 100     │
  │ Budget Adherence      │ 30%    │ (withinBudget / total) * 100 │
  │ Subscription Burden   │ 20%    │ (1 - subCost/income) * 100   │
  │ Spending Consistency  │ 10%    │ (1 - |diff|/max) * 100       │
  └──────────────────────┴────────┴──────────────────────────────┘

  Total Score = sum of (each factor's score * its weight)
  Range: 0 to 100

  Calculation Flow:

  Input: {
    monthlyIncome: 5000,
    monthlyExpenses: 3500,
    monthlySavings: 1500,
    budgetAdherence: 0.8,
    subscriptionBurden: 0.15,
    spendingConsistency: 0.9
  }

  Step 1: Savings Rate
    savingsRate = 1500 / 5000 = 0.3
    savingsScore = 0.3 * 100 = 30
    weighted = 30 * 0.4 = 12

  Step 2: Budget Adherence
    adherenceScore = 0.8 * 100 = 80
    weighted = 80 * 0.3 = 24

  Step 3: Subscription Burden (INVERTED — lower is better)
    burdenScore = (1 - 0.15) * 100 = 85
    weighted = 85 * 0.2 = 17

  Step 4: Spending Consistency
    consistencyScore = 0.9 * 100 = 90
    weighted = 90 * 0.1 = 9

  Total Score = 12 + 24 + 17 + 9 = 62 → "Improving"

  Status Generation:
    Score >= 70 → "Ready"      (student can start paper trading)
    Score >= 40 → "Improving"  (getting there, needs work)
    Score <  40 → "Beginner"   (focus on basics first)

  Reasoning Behind Weights:
    40% Savings: The most important indicator. If you can't save money,
                 you can't invest. Period.
    30% Budget:  Following budgets shows financial discipline — a
                 prerequisite for investment discipline.
    20% Subs:    High subscription burden means less available capital
                 and potentially poor spending habits.
    10% Consistency: Consistent spending patterns indicate financial
                     stability, but it's less critical than the others.

9.6 SERVICE: portfolioValuationService
──────────────────────────────────────

  Portfolio Calculations:

  For each holding:
    costBasis    = quantity * avgBuyPrice       (what you paid)
    currentValue = quantity * currentPrice      (what it's worth now)
    gainLoss     = currentValue - costBasis     (profit or loss)
    gainLossPct  = (gainLoss / costBasis) * 100 (percentage)

  Overall:
    holdingsValue = sum of all currentValues
    portfolioValue = cashBalance + holdingsValue
    totalPnL = holdingsValue - totalCostBasis
    totalPnLPct = (totalPnL / totalCostBasis) * 100

  Graceful Handling of Missing Prices:
    If a stock's price isn't available (Finnhub hasn't sent data yet),
    currentPrice is set to null and currentValue falls back to costBasis.
    This prevents portfolio valuation from breaking during startup.

  Business Logic:
    The service is a pure function — it takes a portfolio object and
    a prices map, and returns computed values. It doesn't read from
    the database or any external source. This makes it:
    1. Testable: Pass mock data, get predictable results
    2. Reusable: Controllers and BullMQ jobs can both call it
    3. Deterministic: Same inputs always produce same outputs


================================================================================
                     SECTION 10: REDIS CONCEPTS USED
================================================================================

10.1 REDIS BASICS
─────────────────

  Redis (Remote Dictionary Server) is an in-memory data structure store.
  It supports strings, lists, sets, hashes, sorted sets, and more.

  Key characteristics:
  - In-memory: All data lives in RAM (extremely fast, microsecond reads)
  - Single-threaded: No lock contention, sequential command execution
  - Persistence optional: Can snapshot to disk (RDB) or log (AOF)
  - Pub/Sub: Built-in publish/subscribe messaging

  In BudgetBuddy, Redis is used for TWO purposes:
  1. Caching (existing): Transaction summaries, subscriptions, overviews
     (config/redis.js → regular GET/SET commands)
  2. Pub/Sub (new): Real-time market data distribution
     (config/redisPubSub.js → PUBLISH/SUBSCRIBE commands)

10.2 PUB/SUB (PUBLISH / SUBSCRIBE)
───────────────────────────────────

  Pub/Sub is a messaging pattern where:
  - Publishers send messages to a CHANNEL (not to specific recipients)
  - Subscribers listen to channels and receive ALL messages published

  It's like a radio station:
  - The DJ (publisher) broadcasts on a frequency (channel)
  - Anyone with a radio (subscriber) tuned to that frequency hears it
  - The DJ doesn't know or care who's listening

  In our implementation:

  Publisher: marketDataGateway.js
    publisher.publish('market:prices', JSON.stringify(priceData))

  Subscriber: server.js
    subscriber.subscribe('market:prices')
    subscriber.on('message', (channel, message) => {
      io.emit('priceUpdate', JSON.parse(message))
    })

  Channel: 'market:prices'

10.3 MESSAGE BROKER CONCEPT
───────────────────────────

  A message broker is an intermediary that receives messages from
  producers and delivers them to consumers. Redis Pub/Sub acts as
  a lightweight message broker.

  In a traditional message broker (like RabbitMQ or Kafka):
  - Messages can be persisted
  - Messages can be acknowledged
  - Failed deliveries can be retried

  Redis Pub/Sub is simpler:
  - Messages are fire-and-forget
  - If no subscriber is listening, the message is lost
  - No message persistence or acknowledgment

  This is FINE for market data because:
  - Price updates are continuous (a lost update is replaced in seconds)
  - We maintain a latestPrices cache as the source of truth
  - Real-time data is inherently ephemeral

10.4 PRODUCER / CONSUMER
─────────────────────────

  Producer: marketDataGateway.js
    Receives price updates from finnhubService (via EventEmitter)
    Produces messages by publishing to Redis channel

  Consumer: server.js (Redis subscriber → Socket.io bridge)
    Subscribes to Redis channel
    Consumes messages by forwarding to Socket.io

  This pattern allows:
  - Adding more consumers without changing the producer
  - Running the producer and consumer on different servers
  - Monitoring the channel independently (redis-cli SUBSCRIBE)

10.5 CHANNELS
─────────────

  A channel is a named message stream in Redis Pub/Sub.

  Our channel: 'market:prices'
  Defined in: config/redisPubSub.js as MARKET_CHANNEL constant

  Message format on this channel:
  {
    "type": "priceUpdate",
    "data": {
      "AAPL": { "price": 300.26, "volume": 40, "timestamp": 1781631022549 }
    },
    "timestamp": 1781631022413
  }

10.6 WHY NOT DIRECT SOCKET.IO BROADCASTING?
────────────────────────────────────────────

  Alternative: finnhubService → directly emit to Socket.io

  Problems with direct approach:

  1. Tight Coupling: finnhubService would need a reference to the io
     object. If Socket.io is refactored, finnhubService must change.

  2. No Horizontal Scaling: With multiple Express servers behind a
     load balancer, each server has its own Socket.io instance. Only
     the server running finnhubService would broadcast updates.

     With Redis: Server 1 (runs finnhub) → publishes to Redis
                 Server 1, 2, 3 all subscribe → all broadcast to clients

  3. No Additional Consumers: Without Redis, adding a BullMQ job or
     analytics pipeline requires modifying finnhubService.

  4. No Debugging: You can't independently monitor the data flow.
     With Redis: redis-cli SUBSCRIBE market:prices shows raw data.

  Interview Answer:
    "I used Redis Pub/Sub instead of direct Socket.io broadcasting
    for three reasons: horizontal scalability, decoupling, and
    observability. With Redis as the intermediary, I can run multiple
    server instances behind a load balancer, each subscribing to the
    same Redis channel. I can add new consumers (like analytics or
    job queues) without modifying the data producer. And I can debug
    the data flow by subscribing to the channel with redis-cli."


================================================================================
                    SECTION 11: WEBSOCKET CONCEPTS
================================================================================

11.1 WHAT ARE WEBSOCKETS?
─────────────────────────

  WebSocket is a communication protocol that provides full-duplex
  (bidirectional) communication over a single TCP connection.

  Unlike HTTP (request-response), WebSocket:
  - Keeps the connection OPEN after the initial handshake
  - Either side can send messages at any time
  - No overhead of HTTP headers on every message
  - Ideal for real-time applications

11.2 DIFFERENCE BETWEEN REST AND WEBSOCKETS
───────────────────────────────────────────

  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
  │ Aspect            │ REST API             │ WebSocket            │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┤
  │ Connection        │ Short-lived          │ Persistent           │
  │ Direction         │ Client → Server      │ Bidirectional        │
  │ Protocol          │ HTTP                 │ ws:// or wss://      │
  │ Headers           │ On every request     │ Only on handshake    │
  │ Latency           │ Higher (new conn)    │ Lower (reuses conn)  │
  │ Server Push       │ Not supported        │ Supported            │
  │ Use Case          │ CRUD operations      │ Real-time updates    │
  │ Scaling           │ Stateless            │ Stateful             │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘

  In InvestLab, BOTH are used:
  - REST: For buy/sell trades, portfolio lookup, readiness score
  - WebSocket: For real-time price updates from Finnhub + to clients

11.3 PERSISTENT CONNECTION
──────────────────────────

  The WebSocket connection between our server and Finnhub stays open
  indefinitely (until network failure or server restart).

  Benefits:
  - No reconnection overhead for each price update
  - Finnhub sends data immediately as trades happen
  - We receive thousands of messages per minute on one connection

  Our Implementation:
  - ws library creates the WebSocket client connection to Finnhub
  - socket.io manages WebSocket connections from browser clients

11.4 EVENT-DRIVEN COMMUNICATION
───────────────────────────────

  Both Finnhub WebSocket and Socket.io use event-driven communication:

  Finnhub sends events:
    { type: 'trade', data: [{ s: 'AAPL', p: 300.26, ... }] }

  Our server emits Socket.io events:
    socket.emit('priceSnapshot', latestPrices)
    io.emit('priceUpdate', updatedPrices)

  Clients listen for events:
    socket.on('priceUpdate', (data) => { /* update UI */ })

11.5 WHY FINNHUB USES WEBSOCKETS
─────────────────────────────────

  Stock prices change hundreds of times per second. If Finnhub used
  REST APIs, clients would need to poll constantly:

  Polling approach:
    Every 1 second: GET /api/prices → response with all prices
    Problem: 86,400 requests per day PER CLIENT
    Problem: Most responses contain NO CHANGES
    Problem: High latency (up to 1 second stale data)

  WebSocket approach:
    One connection: Server pushes ONLY when prices change
    Benefit: Zero wasted requests
    Benefit: Sub-millisecond latency
    Benefit: Lower bandwidth (no HTTP headers on every message)

11.6 WHY SOCKET.IO WAS USED (vs raw WebSockets)
─────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Socket.io provides:
  1. Auto-reconnection: If client disconnects, it reconnects automatically
  2. Fallback: Uses long-polling if WebSocket isn't available
  3. Rooms/Namespaces: Can broadcast to specific groups of clients
  4. Event System: Named events (priceUpdate, priceSnapshot) instead
     of raw message parsing
  5. Binary Support: Can send binary data if needed
  6. Heartbeat: Built-in ping/pong to detect dead connections

  For Finnhub connection we use raw 'ws' library because:
  - It's a server-to-server connection (no browser involved)
  - We don't need reconnection handling from a library (we built our own)
  - Lower overhead than socket.io-client
  - Finnhub's API expects raw WebSocket connections

  Interview Answer:
    "I used Socket.io for server-to-client communication because it
    provides auto-reconnection, fallback to long-polling, and a named
    event system. For the server-to-Finnhub connection, I used the raw
    'ws' library because it's lighter weight and Finnhub expects a
    standard WebSocket connection."


================================================================================
                SECTION 12: EVENT-DRIVEN ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

12.1 EVENTEMITTER
─────────────────

  EventEmitter is a Node.js core module that implements the
  Observer pattern. It allows objects to:
  1. Emit named events
  2. Register listeners for those events
  3. Execute listeners when events are emitted

  In our code:

    // finnhubService.js — EMIT events
    const priceEmitter = new EventEmitter();
    priceEmitter.emit('priceUpdate', updates);

    // marketDataGateway.js — LISTEN for events
    priceEmitter.on('priceUpdate', (updates) => {
      publisher.publish(MARKET_CHANNEL, JSON.stringify({ ... }));
    });

12.2 EVENT PUBLISHING
─────────────────────

  Where events are published:

  1. finnhubService.js emits 'priceUpdate' on priceEmitter
     Trigger: When Finnhub sends a trade message with updated prices
     Data: Object containing only the changed symbols

  2. marketDataGateway.js publishes to Redis 'market:prices'
     Trigger: When it receives 'priceUpdate' from priceEmitter
     Data: JSON payload with type, data, and timestamp

12.3 EVENT CONSUMPTION
──────────────────────

  Where events are consumed:

  1. marketDataGateway.js listens to priceEmitter 'priceUpdate'
     Action: Publishes to Redis Pub/Sub

  2. server.js listens to Redis subscriber 'message' event
     Action: Broadcasts to Socket.io clients

  3. Socket.io clients listen to 'priceUpdate' event
     Action: Update UI (future)

12.4 COMPLETE EVENT FLOW DIAGRAM
────────────────────────────────

  Finnhub Trade Event
         │
         ▼
  finnhubService: ws.on('message')
         │
         │  latestPrices.set(symbol, { price, volume, timestamp })
         │
         ▼
  priceEmitter.emit('priceUpdate', { AAPL: { price: 300.26 } })
         │
         ├──────────────────────────────────────────────┐
         ▼                                              ▼
  marketDataGateway:                              (future consumers:
  priceEmitter.on('priceUpdate')                  analytics, alerting)
         │
         │  publisher.publish('market:prices', payload)
         ▼
  Redis Pub/Sub Channel: 'market:prices'
         │
         ▼
  server.js: subscriber.on('message')
         │
         │  io.emit('priceUpdate', data)
         ▼
  Socket.io Clients (all connected browsers)
         │
         ▼
  React Component (future): socket.on('priceUpdate')

12.5 INTERVIEW RELEVANCE
─────────────────────────

  Event-driven architecture is a top interview topic because:

  1. It demonstrates understanding of the Observer pattern
  2. It shows knowledge of decoupling and loose coupling
  3. It's used by most modern real-time systems
  4. It relates to system design concepts (Kafka, RabbitMQ, etc.)

  Interview Answer:
    "I used event-driven architecture to decouple the market data
    pipeline into three independent stages: ingestion (finnhubService),
    distribution (marketDataGateway via Redis), and delivery (Socket.io
    in server.js). Each stage only knows about events, not about the
    other stages' implementations. This means I can add new consumers
    like analytics pipelines or job queues without modifying existing
    code — I just add another event listener."

13.1 JWT FLOW IN INVESTLAB

    2. jwt.verify(token, JWT_SECRET) → decoded = { id: "6a318866..." }
    3. User.findById(decoded.id).select('-password') → req.user
    4. next()

  Step 5: Controller accesses req.user._id for all database queries

─────────────────────────

  Request
    ↓
  cors()               ← Allow cross-origin
    ↓
  express.json()       ← Parse body
    ↓
  cookieParser()       ← Parse cookies
    ↓
  router.use(protect)  ← JWT verification
    ↓
  getPortfolio()       ← Controller logic
    ↓
  Response

13.3 USER AUTHORIZATION (PORTFOLIO OWNERSHIP)
──────────────────────────────────────────────

  Every controller query is scoped to the authenticated user:

  Portfolio:  Portfolio.findOne({ userId: req.user._id })
  Trades:    Trade.find({ userId: req.user._id })
  Income:    Transaction.aggregate([{ $match: { userId: req.user._id } }])
  Budgets:   Budget.find({ userId: req.user._id })

  This ensures:
  - User A cannot see User B's portfolio
  - User A cannot buy/sell in User B's account
  - User A cannot view User B's trades or readiness score

  There is no role-based access control (RBAC) — all InvestLab users
  have the same permissions. The JWT simply identifies WHO the user is.

13.4 REQUEST LIFECYCLE (COMPLETE)
─────────────────────────────────

  POST /api/investlab/buy (with body: { symbol: "AAPL", quantity: 5 })

  1. TCP connection → Express server (port 5000)
  2. cors() → Check Origin header against CLIENT_URL
  3. express.json() → Parse body → req.body = { symbol: "AAPL", quantity: 5 }
  4. cookieParser() → Parse cookies (not used here)
  5. Route matching → /api/investlab/buy matches investlabRoutes
  6. protect() middleware:
     a. Extract "Bearer eyJ..." from Authorization header
     b. jwt.verify() → { id: "6a318866..." }
     c. User.findById() → req.user = { _id, name, email, xp, level }
     d. next()
  7. buyStock() controller:
     a. Validate symbol, quantity
     b. getLatestPrices() → { AAPL: { price: 300.26 } }

     e. Update holdings (weighted avg)
     f. portfolio.save()
     g. Trade.create()
  8. res.status(201).json({ message, trade, cashBalance })
  9. TCP response → Client

================================================================================
                     SECTION 16: DESIGN DECISIONS
================================================================================

16.1 WHY FINNHUB?
─────────────────

  Options Considered:
  - Alpha Vantage: REST-only, requires polling, lower rate limits
  - Yahoo Finance: Unofficial API, unreliable, rate limited
  - IEX Cloud: Paid for real-time data
  - Finnhub: Free tier with WebSocket support for real-time data

  Decision: Finnhub
  Reasons:
  - Free WebSocket API for real-time trade data
  - Supports up to 50 symbols on free tier (we use 6)
  - Well-documented API
  - Reliable uptime

16.2 WHY WEBSOCKETS (for Finnhub)?
───────────────────────────────────

  Alternative: REST API polling every N seconds

  Tradeoffs:
  ┌──────────────────┬──────────────────────┬──────────────────────┐
  │ Aspect            │ WebSocket            │ REST Polling         │
  ├──────────────────┼──────────────────────┼──────────────────────┤
  │ Latency           │ Milliseconds         │ Seconds (poll freq)  │
  │ Bandwidth         │ Low (data only)      │ High (HTTP overhead) │
  │ API Calls         │ 1 connection         │ N calls/minute       │
  │ Rate Limits       │ Not a concern        │ Easily exceeded      │
  │ Complexity        │ Reconnection logic   │ Simple but wasteful  │
  └──────────────────┴──────────────────────┴──────────────────────┘

  Decision: WebSocket
  For real-time market data, WebSocket is the industry standard.

16.3 WHY REDIS PUB/SUB?
────────────────────────

  Alternative: Direct EventEmitter → Socket.io (no Redis)

  Tradeoffs:
  - Without Redis: Simpler, but limited to single-server deployment
  - With Redis: Slightly more complex, but enables horizontal scaling

  Decision: Redis Pub/Sub
  Even though we're currently single-server, the architecture is
  ready for multi-server deployment. The cost is just one extra
  Redis connection — negligible.

16.4 WHY A SERVICE LAYER?
─────────────────────────

  Alternative: Put all logic in controllers (fat controllers)

  Decision: Service layer
  - readinessEngine is a pure function — easily unit testable
  - portfolioValuationService is reusable by future BullMQ jobs
  - finnhubService encapsulates WebSocket complexity
  - Controllers stay thin and focused on HTTP concerns

16.5 WHY A SEPARATE PORTFOLIOSNAPSHOT MODEL?
────────────────────────────────────────────

  Alternative: Compute historical values from trades on-the-fly

  Decision: Separate model
  - Time-series data needs to be pre-computed and stored
  - Computing portfolio value at past timestamps requires historical
    price data (which we don't persist yet)
  - Snapshots enable O(1) lookups for "what was my portfolio worth
    on June 15?" instead of recomputing from trades + historical prices
  - Will be populated by a BullMQ cron job in a future iteration

16.6 WHY A SEPARATE TRADE COLLECTION?
──────────────────────────────────────

  Alternative: Embed trades inside Portfolio.trades array

  Decision: Separate collection
  - Trade history grows unboundedly (could reach thousands/millions)
  - MongoDB has a 16MB document size limit
  - Trades need independent pagination (skip/limit)
  - Trades are append-only (never updated or deleted)
  - Clean separation: Portfolio = current state, Trades = audit log

================================================================================
            SECTION 19: TECHNICAL INTERVIEW QUESTIONS & ANSWERS
================================================================================

── BACKEND ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How do you keep controllers thin?
A: I separate concerns into layers. Controllers handle HTTP request/response —
   extracting input, calling services, and formatting output. Business logic
   lives in services. For example, my readinessEngine is a pure function that
   takes financial inputs and returns a score. The controller gathers data from
   MongoDB, passes it to the service, and returns the result. This makes the
   service independently testable and reusable.

Q: How do you handle errors in your APIs?
A: Every controller is wrapped in a try/catch block. For validation errors,
   I return specific 400-level status codes with descriptive messages (e.g.,
   400 "Insufficient balance" with the required and available amounts). For
   unexpected errors, I return 500 with the error message. For service
   unavailability (like missing price data), I use 503 to signal a temporary
   condition. I also use fire-and-forget patterns (like Portfolio creation on
   signup) where failure is logged but doesn't block the main flow.

── MONGODB ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why did you use MongoDB instead of PostgreSQL?
A: Three reasons. First, the existing BudgetBuddy codebase uses MongoDB, so
   consistency across the project was important. Second, the Portfolio schema
   benefits from embedding — holdings are stored as subdocuments inside the
   portfolio document, which gives atomic updates when buying/selling stocks.
   In PostgreSQL, this would require a separate holdings table and JOIN
   operations. Third, MongoDB's flexible schema lets me iterate quickly on
   the data model during development.

Q: Explain your indexing strategy.
A: I created compound indexes on { userId: 1, timestamp: -1 } for trades and
   snapshots. The userId field with ascending order supports equality matching
   (find all trades for user X), and timestamp with descending order supports
   sorting (newest first). This follows the ESR (Equality, Sort, Range) pattern
   recommended by MongoDB. For the Portfolio collection, I use a unique index on
   userId to enforce the one-portfolio-per-user business rule and enable O(1)
   lookups.

Q: Why did you embed holdings inside Portfolio instead of a separate collection?
A: Holdings have a bounded cardinality (max 6 symbols), are always accessed with
   the portfolio (never independently), and need atomic updates with cashBalance.
   Embedding avoids joins and provides transactional atomicity — when I buy a
   stock, I update both holdings and cashBalance in a single document write.
   Trades, on the other hand, are in a separate collection because they grow
   unboundedly and need independent pagination.

Q: What is the aggregation pipeline and how did you use it?
A: MongoDB's aggregation pipeline processes documents through sequential stages.
   I used it in the readiness endpoint to compute: (1) $match to filter
   transactions by userId, type, and date range, (2) $group with $sum to
   calculate total income and expenses, (3) $group by category for per-category
   expense totals. The advantage over fetching all documents is that aggregation
   runs on the database server, reducing network transfer and Node.js memory
   usage. I ran multiple aggregations in parallel using Promise.all() for
   optimal performance.

── REDIS ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why do you use two separate Redis connections for Pub/Sub?
A: Redis has a protocol-level limitation: once a connection enters subscriber
   mode (after calling SUBSCRIBE), it can ONLY receive messages — it cannot
   execute commands like GET, SET, or PUBLISH. So I need three connections total:
   one for caching (the existing redis.js client for GET/SET), one for
   publishing (marketDataGateway publishes price updates), and one for
   subscribing (server.js subscribes to receive those updates). The publisher
   and subscriber are in config/redisPubSub.js; the cache client is unchanged
   in config/redis.js.

Q: What happens if Redis goes down?
A: For Pub/Sub: Price updates stop flowing to Socket.io clients. Finnhub
   data is still ingested and cached in the latestPrices Map (in-memory).
   REST API endpoints (buy, sell, portfolio) continue working because they
   read prices from the in-memory cache, not Redis. For caching: The existing
   BudgetBuddy cache (summaries, subscriptions) would fall through to MongoDB
   — higher latency but functional. Both ioredis connections have automatic
   reconnection built in.

Q: Redis Pub/Sub vs Redis Streams vs Kafka — when to use each?
A: Redis Pub/Sub: Fire-and-forget, no persistence, no consumer groups. Perfect
   for real-time price updates where losing one message is fine (the next update
   replaces it). Redis Streams: Persistent, supports consumer groups, at-least-
   once delivery. Use when you need message durability (e.g., order processing).
   Kafka: Distributed, replicated, high throughput, permanent storage. Use for
   large-scale event sourcing, log aggregation, or cross-datacenter replication.
   I chose Pub/Sub because market data is ephemeral — a lost price update is
   replaced by the next one within milliseconds.

── WEBSOCKETS ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How does your WebSocket reconnection work?
A: I use exponential backoff. When the Finnhub WebSocket disconnects, I
   calculate the delay as min(1000 * 2^attempt, 30000). First attempt waits
   1 second, then 2, 4, 8, 16, capped at 30 seconds. On successful connection,
   the attempt counter resets to 0. This prevents overwhelming the server with
   reconnection attempts while ensuring we reconnect quickly after brief
   outages. This is the same pattern used by AWS SDKs and Google API clients.

Q: What's the difference between Socket.io and raw WebSockets?
A: Socket.io wraps WebSockets with additional features: automatic reconnection,
   fallback to HTTP long-polling, named event system (vs raw message strings),
   rooms for targeted broadcasting, binary data support, and heartbeat/ping-pong
   for dead connection detection. I use Socket.io for server-to-client
   communication (browsers) and raw 'ws' library for the server-to-Finnhub
   connection (lower overhead, no browser features needed).

── SOCKET.IO ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: What events does your Socket.io implementation support?
A: Two events: (1) 'priceSnapshot' — emitted once when a client connects,
   containing the latest cached prices for all 6 symbols. This ensures the
   client has data immediately without waiting for the next price change.
   (2) 'priceUpdate' — emitted continuously whenever Redis Pub/Sub receives
   new price data from the marketDataGateway. Contains only the changed
   symbols, not all 6 every time.

Q: How would you add authentication to Socket.io connections?
A: I would use Socket.io middleware. On the server:
   io.use((socket, next) => {
     const token = socket.handshake.auth.token;
     try { jwt.verify(token, JWT_SECRET); next(); }
     catch { next(new Error('Authentication error')); }
   });
   The client passes the token during connection:
   io("http://localhost:5000", { auth: { token: "eyJ..." } });
   This is not implemented yet because Day 1 focuses on infrastructure.

── REST APIs ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How do you handle pagination?
A: I use offset-based pagination with skip and limit. The client sends ?page=2
   &limit=20. The server calculates skip = (page - 1) * limit = 20, then uses
   .skip(20).limit(20). I also return metadata: totalCount (from countDocuments),
   totalPages (Math.ceil(totalCount / limit)), current page, and limit. I cap
   limit at 100 to prevent abuse. The compound index on { userId: 1,
   timestamp: -1 } makes this efficient.

Q: Why cursor-based pagination might be better?
A: Offset pagination has a problem: if new trades are inserted between pages,
   results can shift (duplicates or missed items). Cursor-based pagination uses
   a value from the last item (e.g., timestamp or _id) as the starting point
   for the next page. For trade history, I could use:
   Trade.find({ userId, timestamp: { $lt: lastTimestamp } }).limit(20)
   This is more stable but harder to implement "jump to page 5". For an
   investment-learning module, offset pagination is sufficient.

── JWT ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How does JWT protect InvestLab endpoints?
A: Every InvestLab route applies the 'protect' middleware via router.use(protect).
   This middleware: (1) extracts the Bearer token from the Authorization header,
   (2) verifies it using jwt.verify() with the server's secret key, (3) looks up
   the user in MongoDB using the decoded user ID, (4) attaches the user object to
   req.user. Every controller then uses req.user._id to scope database queries
   to the authenticated user. This prevents User A from accessing User B's data.

Q: What happens when the JWT expires?
A: Access tokens expire after 15 minutes. The client uses the refresh token
   (stored in an httpOnly cookie) to get a new access token via POST
   /api/auth/refresh. The refresh token has a 7-day lifetime. If both expire,
   the user must log in again. InvestLab endpoints return 401 "Not authorized"
   when the token is expired, and the frontend handles the refresh flow.

── SYSTEM DESIGN ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Design a real-time stock price ticker system.
A: I would use this exact architecture:
   1. Data Source: WebSocket connection to a market data provider (Finnhub)
   2. Ingestion Layer: A service that maintains a WebSocket connection, caches
      latest prices, and emits events via EventEmitter
   3. Distribution Layer: Redis Pub/Sub to decouple ingestion from delivery
      and enable horizontal scaling
   4. Delivery Layer: Socket.io for WebSocket connections to browser clients
   5. Storage Layer: MongoDB for portfolios and trades, Redis for caching
   The key insight is using Redis Pub/Sub as the intermediary so multiple
   server instances can share the same market data stream.

Q: How would you handle a flash crash scenario?
A: Several safeguards: (1) Price validation — reject trades if the current
   price deviates more than X% from the previous cached price, (2) Circuit
   breaker pattern — temporarily halt trading if price changes exceed a
   threshold, (3) Rate limiting — prevent rapid-fire buy/sell requests,
   (4) Trade confirmations — show users the execution price before confirming.
   Currently none of these are implemented because it's a learning platform,
   but they would be critical for a production trading system.

── SCALABILITY ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: How would you scale this to 100,000 users?
A: Layered approach: (1) Multiple Express servers behind a load balancer —
   Socket.io uses the Redis adapter for cross-server broadcasting, (2) Finnhub
   ingestion runs as a single dedicated service (one connection is enough),
   (3) MongoDB read replicas for heavy read operations like trade history,
   (4) BullMQ workers for background processing (snapshots, readiness cache),
   (5) CDN for frontend static assets, (6) Socket.io rooms — only broadcast
   to users who have InvestLab open, not all connected users.

Q: What's the bottleneck in your current architecture?
A: At current scale, there's no bottleneck. As users grow, the first bottleneck
   would be Socket.io connections per server (around 10,000). The solution is
   horizontal scaling with Redis adapter. The second bottleneck would be MongoDB
   aggregation queries for readiness scores — solved by caching results in
   Redis or pre-computing via BullMQ jobs.

── ARCHITECTURE ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why not use microservices?
A: For a student project with a small team, microservices add operational
   complexity without proportional benefits. Monolithic with clean module
   boundaries (services layer, clear file structure) gives us the organizational
   benefits of microservices without the deployment, networking, and debugging
   overhead. If the project grew to 5+ developers or needed independent
   deployment cycles, I would extract finnhubService and the readiness engine
   into separate microservices.

Q: Explain the separation between finnhubService and marketDataGateway.
A: Single Responsibility Principle. finnhubService is responsible for connecting
   to Finnhub, parsing trade data, maintaining the price cache, and handling
   reconnection. marketDataGateway is responsible for publishing data to Redis.
   If I want to change how data is distributed (e.g., switch from Redis to
   Kafka), I only change marketDataGateway. If I want to switch market data
   providers (e.g., from Finnhub to IEX), I only change finnhubService. Neither
   knows about the other's implementation.

── PROJECT DECISIONS ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Q: Why did you create the PortfolioSnapshot model on Day 1 if it's not used yet?
A: Forward-thinking design. Creating the model now ensures the schema is defined
   alongside related models (Portfolio, Trade). When I add the BullMQ snapshot
   job later, I won't need to modify existing code or worry about schema
   compatibility. The model file is just a few lines — the cost of creating it
   is negligible, but the cost of retrofitting it later (potential data
   migration, index creation on a large collection) would be higher.

Q: Why fire-and-forget for Portfolio creation on signup?
A: The Portfolio is secondary to the signup flow. If Portfolio creation fails
   (e.g., MongoDB timeout), the user should still be able to sign up and use
   other BudgetBuddy features. The getPortfolio endpoint has lazy initialization
   — it creates the portfolio on first access if it doesn't exist. So even if
   the fire-and-forget fails, the user gets a portfolio the first time they
   visit InvestLab. This is a resilience pattern: non-critical side effects
   should never block the primary operation.

Q: Why did you build InvestLab?
A: I noticed that student finance apps focus only on tracking expenses, but
   never teach students the NEXT step — growing their money. InvestLab bridges
   that gap by first assessing if a student is financially ready to invest
   (using their actual expense and budget data from BudgetBuddy), and then
   providing a safe paper trading environment with real market data. The goal
   is financial education, not stock trading.

Q: What challenges did you face?
A: The biggest challenge was designing the real-time market data pipeline.
   I needed to ensure that Finnhub WebSocket data flowed through multiple
   layers (EventEmitter, Redis Pub/Sub, Socket.io) without introducing
   latency or losing data. The key insight was using Redis Pub/Sub as the
   intermediary layer instead of directly connecting Finnhub to Socket.io.
   This required understanding Redis's limitation that Pub/Sub and regular
   commands can't share a connection. Another challenge was calculating the
   weighted average buy price correctly when a user buys the same stock
   multiple times at different prices.

Q: What would you improve?
A: Three things. First, I'd add a tradeService to move buy/sell business logic
   out of the controller into a proper service layer — currently it's slightly
   fat because validation and business logic are mixed. Second, I'd add Redis
   caching for readiness scores (they involve multiple aggregation queries that
   don't change frequently). Third, I'd implement Socket.io authentication and
   rooms so that price updates are only sent to users who have InvestLab open,
   reducing unnecessary bandwidth.

Q: Why not polling instead of WebSockets?
A: Polling means the client repeatedly asks "any updates?" every N seconds.
   For market data, this has three problems: (1) High latency — if you poll
   every 5 seconds, you could miss price changes that happened in between.
   (2) Wasted requests — most polls return "no change." (3) Higher server
   load — every poll creates a new HTTP connection with full headers.
   WebSockets keep a single persistent connection open, and the server pushes
   data ONLY when something changes. This gives us millisecond latency with
   minimal bandwidth.

Q: Why not SQL (PostgreSQL) instead of MongoDB?
A: MongoDB was chosen for three reasons. First, the existing BudgetBuddy
   codebase already uses MongoDB — using a different database would add
   operational complexity (two databases to manage, backup, monitor). Second,
   the Portfolio schema benefits from MongoDB's embedding — holdings are
   subdocuments inside the portfolio, enabling atomic updates without
   multi-table transactions. Third, the flexible schema lets me iterate
   quickly — I can add fields to Trade or Portfolio without ALTER TABLE
   migrations. That said, PostgreSQL would work well too — especially for
   the Trade collection where strong ACID transactions and complex analytical
   queries would benefit from SQL's strengths.

Q: Why not direct Finnhub → Socket.io? Why the Redis layer in between?
A: Three reasons: scalability, decoupling, and observability. (1) Scalability:
   If I add a second Express server behind a load balancer, only the server
   with the Finnhub connection would receive price data. With Redis, BOTH
   servers subscribe to the same channel and broadcast to their own clients.
   (2) Decoupling: Without Redis, finnhubService needs a reference to the
   Socket.io instance. With Redis, it publishes to a channel and doesn't know
   or care who subscribes. (3) Observability: I can debug the data flow by
   running "redis-cli SUBSCRIBE market:prices" to see raw data without
   touching the application. This is the same architecture used by production
   systems like Slack, Discord, and Robinhood.

Q: How does InvestLab integrate with existing BudgetBuddy features?
A: InvestLab reuses existing data without duplicating it. The readiness
   endpoint queries the Transaction collection (for income/expenses), the
   Budget collection (for budget adherence), and the subscriptionService
   (for subscription burden). It doesn't create separate expense or budget
   tables — it reads directly from the same data the user already manages.
   This means the readiness score updates automatically as the user tracks
   more expenses or sets new budgets. Zero additional data entry required.

Q: What's the most interesting technical decision you made?
A: The readiness scoring engine being a pure function with no database access.
   The controller handles all data gathering (6+ MongoDB queries), then passes
   the aggregated inputs to calculateReadiness(). This means the scoring logic
   is completely independent of the database, the HTTP layer, and the rest of
   the application. I can unit test it with mock inputs, reuse it in a BullMQ
   background job, or even run it on the client side for instant previews.
   This separation of "data gathering" from "computation" is a pattern I
   apply wherever possible.

Q: What did you learn from building this?
A: Three key lessons. (1) Architecture matters more than code: The decision to
   use Redis Pub/Sub between Finnhub and Socket.io was the most impactful
   choice — it affected every other design decision. (2) Separate what changes
   independently: Market data providers might change, scoring algorithms might
   change, delivery mechanisms might change — keeping each in its own service
   file means changes are isolated. (3) Design for the future but build for
   today: I created the PortfolioSnapshot model even though nothing populates
   it yet, because the cost of creating it now is near zero, but the cost
   of retrofitting it later could be significant.
